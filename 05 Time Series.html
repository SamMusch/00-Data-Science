                                Stochastic Processes

                                A stochastic process is a sequence of random variables. A sample path is one sequence of that stochastic process. Because we can't go back in time, a sample path is often all that we have.

                                This is why we need to have stationarity - it allows us to take our sample path and say something about the underlying stochastic process. Stationary is when we are at equilibrium and the errors just bounce around our mean. The expected value at one given point in time is just the average of the different theoretical paths at that time point.

                                Note that seasonality does not imply auto-correlation with previous observation and vice versa

                                • Stationarity

                                  • Constant mean
                                  • Constant sd
                                  • Constant cov
                                  • No seasonality
                                  • Autocorrelation constant

                                • White noise (not predictable): mean of 0, sd of 1, no autocorrelation between lags

                                • Moving average: we do have autocorrelation between lags. We use the previous errors to inform us

                                  • (Example: Assuming iid with mean = 0 and constant variance)

                                  • Expected value = 0

                                     

                                • Not an example of stochastic: random walk

                                  • Random walk: we have autocorrelation and it becomes larger over time

                                    • If we see that a line appears to be trending up or down, it does not necessarily mean that this trending is "actually" occurring; it could just be the result of random walk.

                                    • (Assuming iid with mean = 0 and constant variance)

                                      • Expected value = 0
                                      • Variance today = number of time periods so far * the constant variance
                                      • Covariance = number of time periods the points have in common

                                 

                                Stationary models

                                The change in y is staying the same. This is what allows us to learn from having only one sample path.

                                • Parameters: mean value, mean error, std error, multiplying factor of previous errors
                                • Uses past forecast errors
                                • Variance of the error term changes the scale of the series, but it doesn't change the patterns
                                • We have moving average models for short term dependency, autoregressive for long term dependency

                                 

                                Auto-correlation function (ACF)

                                The ACF function is the correlation between the value today ~ the value from one of the lags. The ACF function shows how many previous time periods have significance in this time period.

                                • If it just tapers off, its probably not a MA model - we use AR instead
                                • If the first 2 bars are very high and the rest are small, we know we have an MA(2) process

                                 

                                Moving Average Models

                                Moving average models are a function of white noise. They use the "errors" from the past periods to inform the value today.

                                (note that x is a learned parameter, its how far "off" we were in the previous period)

                                Using MA(2)

                                X1=errorfromyesterdayX2=errorfromdaybefore
                                E(today)=0Variance(today)=1+x12+x22
                                Cov(today,yesterday)=−x1+x1∗x2Cov(today,daybeforeyesterday)=−x2

                                 

                                Autoregressive models

                                Autoregressive models are a function of their past.

                                Long term dependency

                                • Polyroot() function checks for stationary

                                  • Stationary when |allroots|>=1

                                 

                                Note that X is the coefficient for the previous days and should have an abs less than 1 for stationary

                                Using AR(1)

                                Valuetoday=X∗yesterday+whitenoise
                                E(today)=0Variance(today)=11−x2
                                Cov(today,yesterday)=x1−x2Cov(today,daybeforeyesterday)=x21−x2

                                Backshift

                                We have a backshift operator if backshift∗valuetoday=Valueyesterday

                                AR(1)model:Valuetoday=X∗(backshift∗valuetoday)+whitenoise

                                 

                                Whitenoise=(1−X∗backshift)∗valuetoday

                                We can replace the middle part to make things easier

                                Whitenoise=Z(backshift)∗valuetoday

                                 

                                ARMA

                                Autoregress + moving avg

                                Taylor expansion, best linear approximation of any stationary process

                                 

                                X = coef for autoregressive

                                M = coef for moving average white noise

                                Valuetoday=Xyesterday+...+Mtoday+Myesterday...

                                 

                                Non-stationary

                                With stationarity, correlation between time points is constant

                                Converting RW to stationary -- take the log, if that doesnt work then take the lag instead

                                We prefer to not use differencing unless we need to

                                Second difference = (1 - backshift operator) ^ 2

                                 

                                Yt = t + e1 is not stationary because the mean will change, but the moving average will be stationary

                                 

                                Polynomial (higher order) can be used, but will hurt us in forecasting

                                 

                                Differencing

                                Randomwalk:Firstdifference=Valuetoday−valueyesterday

                                Even though random walk is not stationary, the first difference is.

                                Firstdifference=1−backshift

                                Seconddifference=(1−backshift)2

                                Model Selection

                                1. ADF

                                We use this to determine whether we have stationarity. If we fail to reject, then we have to use ARIMA. When the adf.test() gives us the result of rejecting the null, then we have stationarity. We can then look into seeing if we should use AR, MA, ARMA.

                                 

                                We can use the following models if we do have stationarity.

                                1. ACF

                                We use this to determine if we have a pure Moving Average model. We should use the time periods who have stat significance.

                                 

                                1. PACF

                                We use this to see if we have a pure Auto Regressive model. We calculate the correlation by using the residuals after we have run a linear regression. We use the "second" image and then interpret in the same way as we had done with the ACF.

                                 

                                1. EACF

                                If we don't have a pure AR model or MA model, we use this to determine the correct number of days to use for our ARMA model. We select the circle that is at the top-left of the model.

                                 

                                1. AIC, BIC

                                We use these to compare the different models. Both incur a cost for the number of parameters (time periods) to include in the model. BIC has a larger penalty for many parameters, so will produce a more simple model. Max likelihood is the coef value that was most likely to generate the data that we have.

                                AIC - Akaike - Model with lowest AIC is best

                                AIC=−2log(maxlikelihood)+2∗numberofparameters

                                 

                                BIC - Bayesian - Same as AIC but penalizes extra model parameters by including sample size in the cost function - Model with lowest is best

                                BIC=−2log(maxlikelihood)+numberofparameters∗log(samplesize)

                                 

                                 

                                Forecasting

                                • Naive - use yesterday

                                • Average - use x number of days

                                • Simple exponential smoothing - same thing as ARIMA(0, 1, 1)

                                  • Valuetomorrow=.9∗today+.9∗.11∗yesterday+.9∗.12∗thedaybeforethat
                                  • We can use special cases of this - Holt's Linear, Holt-Winters

                                • Forecasting with AR(1) --- "x" is the value that tells us how much of the prior day to use. Not great for long term forecasting. When we have a non-zero mean, just use the average to center our values around the mean.

                                  • Valuetomorrow=x1+whitenoise
                                  • Valuein4days=x4+whitenoise

                                • Forecasting with MA(1) -- "x" is how far off we were today

                                  • Valuetomorrow=−x∗(expectedvaluetoday−actualvaluetoday)
                                  • Valuein4days=ourmean

                                 

                                 

                                Trends

                                Deterministic trend - the average is not staying the same, going up or down. Trend could be linear, polynomial, seasonal, hybrid. If the trend is constant (flat), then we still have stationarity.

                                Value=trendfunction+stationarynoise

                                We have positive correlation when one point is high, and the next one is as well. When this happens, our estimate of the mean is off.

                                When we have negative correlation instead, we have a good estimate of the mean but our variance is underestimated.

                                Ways to test if we actually have a trend, or just random walk

                                • ADF test - a high p-value means we do not have linear trend

                                • Residual analysis

                                  • if the residuals are correlated, its likely that we do not have linear trend
                                  • qqplot - if the points don't fit the line, we do not have linear trend
